The Boss

Meet the Boss. He is a strict but fair leader who expects peak performance from his JavaScript employees. To manage the office efficiently, he issues every instruction using the addEventListener() command, which tells his staff exactly which "event" to watch for. Inside the parentheses of this command, the Boss outlines the specific task to be performed, finalized by his official signature: "});". This signature acts as a seal, signaling that the instruction is complete and ready for filing. Below the executive suite, the employees wait patiently, prepared to carry out their assigned duties the moment the Boss issues a command.

The Break

With all this work, it's time for a break! As the clock ticks down every Javascript employee is also 'listening' for the time to reach their break period. Once on their break the employee switches from work mode to relaxation mode, just like the light and dark mode for a website or app! Wait... even though an employee is on break they are still an employee, right? How do we know this? Every employee has an assigned role called a variable. Generally, their role as an employee is constant, or for short, 'const'. However, their role in the team changes during break. Someone assigned to be working on a report is now assigned to give themself some time to rest. This means the boss is 'letting' them perform a temporary role that changes when they are back on the clock. Wow that was a lot of text, give yourself a quick break from reading before scrolling!

The Exit

All that work was tiring! It's time for the Javascript employees to clock-out for the day. After recording their time on the timesheet, the employee's hours are placed in filing or 'localStorage'. This localStorage makes sure that the time an employees work is remembered each time they visit and exit the building, just like a user's preferences on a website. At the end of their pay period, the employee is paid for the time they have worked unless they did not work at all. This can be represented as something called a conditional. For example, if an employee works, they will be paid. If anything else, meaning no work has occured, the employee will not be paid. Together, localStorage and conditionals ensure that Javascript employees can leave work with peace of mind.

LocalStorage.setItem("hours", "16");, if (hours>=1){ console.log("paid");}
